CLINICAL CODER

WHAT IT'S LIKE BEING A CLINICAL CODER

    A clinical coder's day typically involves reviewing medical records, assigning alphanumeric codes to diagnoses and procedures, ensuring accuracy and compliance with coding guidelines, collaborating with healthcare providers to clarify documentation, and staying updated on coding regulations and changes. It's detail-oriented work crucial for healthcare billing and data analysis.

    To become a clinical coder, one typically needs a strong understanding of medical terminology, anatomy, and physiology. Many clinical coders have a background in healthcare, such as nursing or medical assisting, but it's not always required. Formal education through a certificate program or associate's degree in health information management or medical coding can be beneficial. Additionally, obtaining certification through organizations like the American Academy of Professional Coders (AAPC) or the American Health Information Management Association (AHIMA) can enhance job prospects and demonstrate proficiency in coding standards.

HOW MUCH DO THEY EARN

    The salary for clinical coders can vary based on factors such as location, experience, education, and employer. In the United States, clinical coders typically earn an average salary ranging from $40,000 to $70,000 per year. However, those with specialized certifications or extensive experience may earn higher salaries, potentially exceeding $80,000 or more annually.

    The estimated total pay range for a Clinical Coder at NHS is £21K–£26K per year, which includes base salary and additional pay. The average Clinical Coder base salary at NHS is £22K per year. The average additional pay is £0 per year, which could include cash bonus, stock, commission, profit sharing or tips.

WHAT CAREERS ARE THERE?

    Aspiring clinical coders have several job options in the healthcare industry. Some of these include:
    1. Clinical Coder:The primary role involves reviewing patient medical records and assigning appropriate codes for diagnoses, procedures, and services according to coding guidelines and standards.
    2. Medical Records Coder:Similar to a clinical coder, but may focus specifically on coding medical records for billing and reimbursement purposes.
    3. Inpatient Coder: Specialises in coding medical records for patients admitted to hospitals or other inpatient healthcare facilities.
    4. Outpatient Coder: Specialises in coding medical records for patients receiving healthcare services on an outpatient basis, such as physician visits, diagnostic tests, and outpatient surgeries.
    5. Specialty Coder: Focuses on coding for specific medical specialties, such as cardiology, orthopaedics, oncology, or obstetrics/gynaecology.
    6. Auditor:Conducts audits of coded medical records to ensure accuracy, completeness, and compliance with coding guidelines and regulations.
    7. Coding Educator/Trainer: Provides coding education and training to healthcare providers, clinical coders, and other healthcare professionals to improve coding accuracy and compliance.
    8. Coding Supervisor/Manager:Oversees a team of clinical coders, provides coding guidance and support, and ensures coding processes are efficient and compliant.
    9. Risk Adjustment Coder: Specialises in coding medical records for risk adjustment purposes, such as for Medicare Advantage or other value-based payment programs.
    10. Remote Coder:Works remotely, often as a contractor or freelance coder, coding medical records for various healthcare organizations or coding companies.
